User Experience Design

Canadian Tire - Automotive

Role

UX Design Lead

Start

April 2018

Launch

June 2019

Website
canadiantire.com

01.-

Overview

Introduction

Canadian Tire (TSX: CTC) is one of the largest retailers in Canada, selling a wide selection of products, from home goods and sports and recreation to hardware and automotive. Over the years, the company had evolved from a brick and mortar retailer to a competitive e-commerce website.

I was tasked with updating the user experience of the Canadian Tire website, specifically the Automotive department pages. Due to the complex nature of selling automotive products, the project was not only a challenge, but also an incredibly rewarding experience. Representing the user, I lead the team through research, user-testing and provided final designs and deliverables.

The project was managed using Agile project management. It was lead by product owners and included a team of business analysts, project managers, engineers, designers, QA and developers. Half of our team worked out of the Toronto head office, while the other half worked overseas.

The first project that I worked on for the Automotive department was focused specifically on the tires and wheels purchasing experience. After the project went live, we saw a significant increase online sales for tires and wheels. Due to the success of the project, we were tasked with implementing and building upon those earlier designs for the rest of the Automotive department.

02.-

Project Goals

Empower all types users to feel confident purchasing automotive products online:

How do we make sure that all types of customers, from DIYers, to someone with limited knowledge about cars, have a frictionless experience purchasing products for their vehicle?

There is added complexity to purchasing automotive products because it’s important that the product has the right fit. In many cases, a product has almost endless variations depending on the year, make, model, body and options of the vehicle. Each product provides unique challenges and we had to account for them when designing large-scale solutions.


Improve the overall customer experience of purchasing automotive products:

The team and I noticed the different points in the experience where users were dropping off. We wanted to make sure that we addressed these problem areas and provided useful solutions. For example, how do we communicate to customers that when a product is out of stock online, they are still able to call and place an order with a representative?

03.-

Our Process

Using our toolbox

The project involved various products and areas of the Canadian Tire website. We made significant updates to the Search Results Page (SRP), Product Description Page (PDP) and the cart.

I lead daily UX touchpoints where team members were invited to brainstorm, validate ideas and test out assumptions. We also worked with the larger UX project team to complete competitive analysis, usability testing and prototyping.

Competitive Analysis
Viewport Analysis

Research Methods

We used the following techniques to learn more about our users and validate decisions.

Competitive Analysis

For each design challenge, we would research competitors and compare their solutions.

 

We also looked at overall site goals and measured our designs against site-wide documentation, such as the viewport analysis. Given the complexity of automotive products, we had to break down which items were the most important and what the hierarchy of information should be on pages like the PDP.  

Prototyping

Prototyping gave us the opportunity to test out some big ideas. We dove deeper into new experiences that were not already on the website and used our prototypes to validate designs and illustrate ideas.

Two main areas of focus were the vehicle bar and product bundles like wiper blades or tire/wheel packages.

User Testing

We used usertesting.com for a wide variety of user tests from understanding how users shopped using the current website, to validating new design decisions. This gave us a wide understanding of how users shop for wiper blades and what they expect to see on the SRP, PDP and once they add products to their cart. A notable number of users did not understand that they needed to purchase the two blades separately. This lead to them checking out with only one blade in their cart. These tests also gave us insight into other areas that we should focus on, like taxonomy. For example, when users searched for windshield wipers, we made sure to display wiper blade results.  

A/B Testing

Since the design changes that we made for Tires and Wheels were already in production when we designed the rest of the automotive depart, we were able to run A/B tests to validate future design decisions. Some of the tests that we ran included: adding quick ‘Add to Carts’ on SRP Tiles and adding ‘Out of Stock Messaging’ to SRP Tiles. In addition, there was an overall A/B testing strategy for the entire site and we referenced findings from those tests in our designs.

04.-

Conclusion

In Summary,

This project was an incredible opportunity to advocate for the user and implement design changes. Our projects had a positive effect on the overall Canadian Tire website and since completing the project, design decisions that we made for the automotive department were adapted to the rest of the site. This included our minimalistic approach to SRP tiles and our highly-optimized PDP. Changes to the PDP were estimated to result in millions of dollars in incremental sales based on how well it did during A/B testing.  

This project allowed me to dig deep into an area of sales that I did not have much experience in. I relied heavily on UX research tools to gain a thorough understanding of our customers shop for automotive products. The collaboration within our team and with other stakeholders was fantastic. The product owners were incredibly involved in all UX conversations and the agile work environment allowed our team to communicate effectively.
